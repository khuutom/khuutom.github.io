Brigham and Women's Hospital

Mechanical Engineering Research Coop

The Traverso Lab at Brigham and Women's Hospital, MIT, and Harvard Medical School is a research program focused on developing next-generation drug delivery systems as well as biomedical devices to support physiological sensing for safe and efficient therapeutics in the gastrointestinal tract. Giovanni Traverso, M.D., Ph.D. is principal investigtator.
My role was to use computer simulation and wet lab experimental testing to develop e-GLUE, a novel, electrically responsive hydrogel. This biocompatible material becomes adhesive when introduced to an electric signal. Applications for this hydrogel in the GI tract include hemostasis bandages to promote healing, drug delivery patches for localized efficacy, and biosensing device platforms for long-term retention.
I used COMSOL Multiphysics to visualize the effects of electric current and fields of various mechanical designs and determine the configuration for maximum adhesion and minimum temperature increase. I then designed and performed experiments to verify these findings in-vitro and characterize the e-GLUE performance using an Instron tensile tester. Experimentation progressed to ex-vivo, then in-vivo with swine animal models. In-vivo x-ray testing saw an improvement of initially less than 7 day retention in the stomach to 30 day retention using improved electrodes and techniques. Experimentation progressed again to include a drug delivery study to collect data on drug presence in the blood over time. I produced a standard calibration curve for HPLC chemical analysis to prepare for upcoming blood samples. This data collection is ongoing and will be included in a future published article.
Unique skills were required for this position. I utilized COMSOL to build simulations, Solidworks and AutoCAD to model the electrodes, laser cutting to manufacture parts, wet lab equipment to synthesize hydrogels, biosafety and chemical hoods for handling material, and experimental methods from in-vitro to ex-vivo and in-vivo.
Above all, I met a diverse group of researchers originating from all over the world. I listened to many stories and got to know many personalities. While much of the work was independent and self-driven, I was motivated by brilliant people around me. This was my introduction to the limitless world of medical research, and I am a more curious learner because of it.

Academic Projects

I am grateful to have the opportunity to contribute to meaningful projects in and out of the classroom. Projects span from high school to the present. My work has taught me invaluable lessons on experimentation, design, and manufacturing.

Custom RC Plane

I was introduced to RF communication and PCB design during my coop at BWH. Although it was not a part of my job responsibility, I took a great interest in the technology and manufacturing methods. This RC plane is a fun way to learn more about these techniques and demonstrate a range of engineering skills. My goal was to create an aerial vehicle under 2cuft with the longest outdoor flight time. The PCB design evolved from hand-drawn circuit schematics to polished files in Altium. The frame was designed from scratch in Solidworks and structurally analyzed in ANSYS. Fluid dynamics analysis for the airfoils were completed in ANSYS and MATLAB. Mechanical design was improved and iterated based on these analyses. I started in August 2023 and hope to begin manufacturing before October 2023.

Barecka Lab - Northeastern

Our group performs research that enables a carbon-neutral future. We develop a fundamental understanding of CO2 electrolysis, employ chemical analysis, and develop in-house models to work towards this goal. Current systems in the lab form complex chemical building blocks such as ethylene via electroreduction of dilute CO2. My contribution is to improve an existing carbon capture reactor design, then take what I have learned to create and test new designs that optimize yield results. I joined the lab in August 2023.

Multiphase Transport Lab - Northeastern

Our group studies the physics of fluid interactions to develop design solutions for challenges in manufacturing, environment, and healthcare sectors. A partner and I studied the Leidenfrost effect, which describes the low friction interaction between a cold fluid and hot surface. Our goal was to design a test apparatus that enables high-speed imaging of drop behavior upon contact of a precisely heated substrate. We completed a literature review and modeled an apparatus that featured four heat cartridges, a control unit, and k=type thermocouples. I completed an ANSYS heat simulation to visualize the contact point and determine the appropriate heating cartridge power rating. I Joined the lab in November 2022 and passed the project onto another student in January 2023.

AeroNu Rocket

Northeastern's aerospace and rocketry club is a student-led organization hosting projects ranging from mothership rotary drones to Karman Line rockets or fixed-wing vehicles. My freshman year, I joined a team of three tasked with designing, building, testing, and launching a rocket. The design specifications were to carry a 5lb payload over 1000ft and collect flight data on both ascent and descent. My individual role was to engineer the interfaces between sections of the rocket and ensure precise fitment while enabling sucessful stage separation. I was also responsible for design of a motor section capable of transferring power from a J-class solid rocket motor. We used Solidworks, OpenRocket, and GrabCAD as the main software packages throughout the process. The project ended with the launch of Needle, our 7ft tall 4in diameter custom rocket, in May of 2021 at Amesbury MA. The launch was a success! We analyzed flight data afterwards and determined that large rockets designed in the future should be balanced with a stability ratio closer to 1 cal. Needle flew with over-stability, which introduced unwanted lateral oscillations.

Cornerstone CovidBot

Cornerstone of Engineering is a hands-on introductory course for first year students. I joined a team of three tasked with designing a robot that dispenses and collects Covid-19 PCR test tubes on tables in the Northeastern Testing Center. I used AutoCAD and Solidworks to initially model the robot, then integrated custom laser-cut components with Arduino kit parts to create the robot. Software was written in C++ with Arduino. DC motors and servos enabled navigation on the table and actuation of the dispensing mechanism. A promotional video was recorded on a DSLR, and I edited the final submission in Adobe Premiere.

Assistive Liftware

Seven Hills Foundation provides clinical, educational, and community support for children and adults with disabilities or significant life challenges. In 2019, I partnered with a classmate to provide an engineering solution for individuals at Seven Hills suffering from neuro-generative disease that makes eating difficult. Existing products, such as LiftWare, stabilize eating utensils using gyroscopic sensors and motors. However, our client had difficulty grasping the unaccommodating handle shape. I used Solidworks to model organic surfaces of the LiftWare device, then I designed an attachment that was more comfortable for the client to hold. Additionally, the solution was easily attached and removed, durable, and did not permanently alter the LiftWare device. We used Makerbot 3D FDM printers to rapidly prototype. This was my first formal exposure to the engineering cycle and 3D modeling. The experience of working on the project and hearing positive feedback from the client inspired my university studies.
